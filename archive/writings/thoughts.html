Thinking 1 Feb 2025

The 80/20 rule is obvious and nobody actually uses it

Most people have heard of the Pareto principle — 80% of outcomes come from 20% of causes. It gets referenced a lot, usually in productivity articles between a section on cold showers and a section on journaling. The thing is, it's genuinely one of the more useful mental models out there, and most people treat it as trivia rather than a tool.

The honest application of it is uncomfortable. It means that if you have ten things on your to-do list, two of them matter significantly more than the other eight combined. That's a confronting thought, because it implies the other eight are mostly noise — or at least, not the highest-leverage use of your time. Most people know this and do the eight things anyway, because the eight things are easier to tick off, and ticking things off feels like progress.

The same pattern shows up everywhere once you look for it. A small number of customers generate most of the revenue. A handful of decisions in any project define most of the outcome. A few habits — sleep, movement, diet — account for the bulk of how you feel day to day. The 80% of effort that goes into the remaining 20% of results isn't wasted exactly, but it is worth interrogating whether it's where you actually want to be spending yourself.

The hard part isn't identifying the 20%. Usually you already know roughly what it is. The hard part is doing less of the comfortable, low-stakes stuff and spending that time on the things that are harder, less immediately satisfying, and more consequential. That's not a productivity tip. It's just a kind of discipline that most advice dresses up to look easier than it is.

Food 15 Jan 2025

Australian food is good because Australia stopped pretending it had its own cuisine

For a long time, the honest answer to "what is Australian food?" was a slightly embarrassed shrug. Meat pies. Vegemite. Snags on a barbecue. Which are fine, but they're not exactly a culinary identity to build a flag around. The interesting thing is that at some point, Australia quietly stopped trying to have a traditional cuisine and just became really good at taking other people's food seriously.

The result is one of the more genuinely interesting food cultures in the world. The Vietnamese community in Melbourne alone has produced a banh mi culture that rivals anything you'd find in Saigon. The Lebanese influence on Sydney's late-night food is substantial and largely underappreciated. Japanese techniques have filtered into the way a lot of modern Australian chefs think about fish. Italian food has been so thoroughly absorbed that most Australians don't think of pasta as a foreign thing at all — it's just Tuesday.

What makes it work is that it's not fusion for its own sake. It's not "Vietnamese-Italian" as a gimmick. It's more that a diverse population brought their food with them, found good local produce to work with, and the result was additive rather than diluted. A proper laksa in Melbourne is a proper laksa. A souvlaki in Oakleigh tastes like it belongs there. The cuisines haven't been Australian-ified into blandness — they've been given room to be themselves in a new place.

The coffee culture is its own separate argument, but it belongs in this conversation too. The flat white exists because Australians were dissatisfied with what was available and decided to make it better. That attitude — take the thing, take it seriously, improve it if you can, leave it alone if you can't — is probably the closest thing to a philosophy that Australian food actually has.
