Project

Flox

A small domain-specific language for describing flow networks and simulating how stuff moves through them over time. Today it's rivers and dams. Tomorrow, Markov chains, traffic, and supply chains, all sharing one parser and one engine.

Javatree-walking interpreterplaceholder06-05-2026

The pitch

Flow networks show up everywhere. Water through a catchment. Probability through a Markov chain. Cars through intersections. Goods through warehouses. They all share the same shape: nodes connected by directed edges, with stuff being redistributed each tick.

Flox is a tiny language that lets you describe one with an arrow operator and a few brackets, then watch it run. The river preset comes first because the dynamics are concrete and the visualisation is fun. Other modes plug into the same engine.

A taste

system Canberra;

Googong[2, 2.0, 0.7] -> googongDam{1000, 80%, 0.8, 1.2} -> Queanbeyan[3, 1.5];
upperMongolo[3, 1.8, 0.6] -> Queanbeyan[3, 1.5];
Queanbeyan[3, 1.5] -> centralMongolo[];
centralMongolo[] -> burleyGriffinDam{5000, 60%, 0.9, 1.5} -> lowerMongolo[];

map Canberra;
print Canberra 10;

Square brackets are rivers (baseFlow, peakMultiplier, decayRate). Curly brackets are dams (capacity, threshold, normalRate, openRate). Empty brackets are outlets. The -> says water flows left to right.

Where we are

Now Working scanner, parser, AST and tree-walking interpreter in Java. River hydrograph simulation, network map, per-day flow tables. Known rough edges: dam units bug, no comments, positional params only.
Next Harden the river language. Comments, named parameters, declare-once references, fix the dam units bug, rainfall as a series instead of a scalar, a golden-file test harness.
Then Port the interpreter to TypeScript. Ship a browser playground: Monaco editor, D3 graph, Recharts hydrograph, shareable permalinks.
After Generalise the engine with a mode switch and edge weights. Same parser, different tick rule. First non-river demo: a Markov chain.
Eventually Three.js renderer with procedural terrain. More modes: traffic, SIR epidemics, supply chains, pressure-driven plumbing.
