Methodology.

How the World Cup 2026 numbers on the main page are produced. Each section gives the plain-English version first, then the technical detail underneath. The code and the tuned parameters stay private; the reasoning does not.

Match results → Elo ratings → Match model → 10,000 simulations → Probabilities

The top-scorer prediction runs on a separate track from club form.

01Data

Everything the model knows, it learned from results that already happened. No opinions, no bought-in rankings, no eye test.

Under the hood

The core training set is every senior men's international result the database holds, going back decades: date, teams, score, venue, competition type. Three further sources feed specific parts of the pipeline:

  • Published squad lists and recent club appearances, scraped per team.
  • Historical World Cup top scorers, used to fit league-strength multipliers, never as goal counts directly.
  • Bookmaker outright prices, used only as an after-the-fact sanity check, never as a model input.

02Team rating: Elo

Every team carries one number for how strong it is right now. Beat someone better than you and it climbs; lose to someone worse and it falls. Simple, and it travels well across eras.

Under the hood

The rating is an Elo, the same family used in chess and FiveThirtyEight's old soccer model. After each match a team's rating moves by an amount set by the result, the goal margin, and how surprising the outcome was.

# standard Elo update, applied after every match
expected = 1 / (1 + 10 ** ((opp_rating - team_rating) / scale))
team_rating += K * margin_mult * (result - expected)

# K, scale, the margin multiplier and the recency
# half-life are all tuned on held-out data, not shown.

Two adjustments earned their place in validation:

  • Friendlies are weighted less than competitive matches. A friendly drubbing of a B-team should not move ratings the way a qualifier loss does.
  • Recent matches count more than old ones, but only mildly. Heavy recency weighting performed worse than a long memory.

03Match model

Give the model two teams and it does not just name a winner. It produces a full spread of plausible scorelines, 2-1, 0-0, the occasional 4-0, at realistic frequencies.

Under the hood

A match is drawn as a single joint scoring process, not two independent Poisson draws. Real football goals are correlated: a chasing team concedes more, a leading team shuts up shop. The joint sampler reproduces that; independent goals do not.

# one correlated draw, not two independent Poissons
home_goals, away_goals = joint_sample(
    rating_gap,
    home_advantage,   # calibrated from the result database
    altitude_bump,    # high-elevation Mexican venues
    temperature,      # shapes the score distribution
)

Three layers sit on top of the rating gap:

  • Home advantage calibrated on the actual home edge in the result database, not a guessed constant.
  • Altitude bump for matches at high-elevation Mexican venues, where visiting sides historically underperform.
  • Temperature on the scoring distribution, fitted so predicted score frequencies match observed ones.

04Squad layer

A nation's rating can drift away from who actually turns up. Stars retire, a wonderkid breaks through, the first-choice striker limps off in May. This layer corrects for that.

Under the hood

It adjusts the national-team rating using the current club form of each named player in the published squad.

It is deliberately held out of the live model for now. Adjusting on partial squad lists biases the table toward whichever countries announced first; the layer switches on once enough squads are public for the comparison to be fair.

05Tournament simulation

The model plays the entire World Cup, every group game and every knockout, ten thousand times over. How often a team reaches each stage becomes its probability.

Under the hood

Each run plays the group stage, computes standings, resolves the best-third qualifiers, and runs the bracket including extra time and penalty shootouts.

for trial in range(10_000):
    standings  = play_group_stage()
    qualifiers = standings + best_thirds(standings)
    champion   = run_knockouts(qualifiers)   # ET, then pens
    record(trial, rounds_reached)

probabilities = average_over_trials()

The Table shows probabilities averaged across all ten thousand runs. The Group and Knockout tabs show one single run, chosen to sit near the median chaos level so a typical number of upsets survives. Picking one real run, rather than stitching together the most likely result of each tie, keeps the two views internally consistent.

06Top scorer

Who wins the Golden Boot is its own question. The model builds each contender's goal tally from how much he plays, how prolific he is for his club, where he plays, and how strong his league is.

Under the hood

Club scoring rate comes from 2025-26 season data; position sets a role weight. League strength is fitted empirically: for every past World Cup, the share of top-ten finishers from a league is compared with that league's share of forwards in tournament squads. Over-delivering leagues earn a multiplier above one, under-delivering leagues below one, with empirical-Bayes shrinkage so thin samples are pulled toward neutral.

expected_wc_goals = (
      minutes_share        # how much he is on the pitch
    * club_scoring_rate    # 2025-26 club goals per 90
    * role_weight          # forward, attacking mid, ...
    * league_multiplier    # empirically fitted, per league
    * environment_scaler   # lifts totals into historic range
)
# every weight and multiplier is fitted; values stay private.

The final environment scaler lifts absolute totals so the predicted winner lands inside the historical Golden Boot range rather than below it.

07Validation

None of this is worth anything if it cannot be checked. Every change to the model has to prove itself by re-predicting tournaments that have already happened.

Under the hood

The full pipeline is back-tested on sixteen past tournaments, World Cups, Euros, Copa Américas, AFCONs and Asian Cups, predicting each match using only data available before kickoff. The full grid is roughly 1,400 evaluations.

loss = 0
for tournament in past_16:   # WCs, Euros, Copas, AFCONs, Asian Cups
    for match in tournament:
        p = model.predict(match, using=data_before(match))
        loss += log_loss(p, actual_result)

# a change ships only if it lowers loss on matches
# it was never tuned on.

The metric is log-loss against the actual result. A change is kept only if it lowers log-loss on the held-out grid. Travel fatigue, deeper recency weighting and Bayesian in-tournament updating all looked sensible and all failed this test.

08What was deliberately left out

Some ideas sound right and still do not help. These were built, tested, and dropped.

  • Travel fatigue. No measurable effect after controlling for opponent strength.
  • Player form via match-by-match scraping. Noise dominated the signal at the granularity tested.
  • Continental-host edges. Already captured by the home-advantage and altitude layers.
  • Bayesian in-tournament updating. Group-stage results are too few to update ratings reliably; the prior dominates anyway.

09Known limitations

Where the model is weakest, stated plainly.

  • Top-scorer training data is survivor-biased: it only sees players who actually went to the World Cup. Non-Big-5 leagues have small samples, which is why their multipliers are shrunk hard.
  • The home-advantage figure is averaged across host nations and competitions. A specific host's edge could be larger or smaller.
  • Squad data is only as current as the published rosters. Late injuries are not reflected unless squads are re-scraped.
  • The model has no view on managerial changes, internal squad dynamics, or the specific tactical match-ups that often decide knockout games.
