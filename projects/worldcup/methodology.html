Methodology.

How the World Cup 2026 numbers on the main page are produced. Concepts only. The code is private; this page exists so the claims can be evaluated on their reasoning.

Data

The model is trained on every senior men's international result the database has, going back decades. Four sources feed it:

  • International match results (date, teams, score, venue, competition type).
  • Club rosters and recent appearances, scraped per published squad.
  • Historical World Cup top scorers, used to fit league-strength multipliers, not goal counts directly.
  • Bookmaker outright prices, used only as a sanity check, never as an input.

Team rating: Elo

Each team carries an Elo rating, the same family of rating used in chess and FiveThirtyEight's old soccer model. A team's rating moves up when it beats stronger opposition and down when it loses to weaker opposition, with the size of the move set by the result, the score margin, and how surprising the result was.

Two adjustments matter:

  • Friendlies are weighted less than competitive matches. A friendly drubbing of a B-team should not move ratings the way a qualifier loss does.
  • Recent matches count more than old ones, but only mildly. Heavy recency weighting performed worse in validation than a long memory.

Match model

Given two ratings, a match is simulated as a joint scoring process rather than two independent Poisson draws. Goals in real football are correlated (a team chasing concedes more, leading teams shut up shop), and the joint sampler reproduces that better than independent goals.

On top of the rating difference, three layers apply:

  • Home advantage calibrated on the actual home edge in the result database, not a guessed constant.
  • Altitude bump for matches at high-elevation Mexican venues. Visiting sides historically underperform there.
  • Temperature on the scoring distribution, fitted to make predicted score frequencies match observed ones.

Squad layer

National-team rating drifts from squad reality between tournaments. Players retire, new ones break through, key forwards get injured. A squad layer adjusts the rating using current club form of each named player in the published squad. It is held back from the live model pending more squads being announced, because adjusting on partial rosters skews toward whichever country announced first.

Tournament simulation

The tournament is simulated end to end ten thousand times. Each simulation plays every group game, computes standings, resolves best-third qualifiers, and runs the knockout bracket including extra time and penalty shootouts.

The table shows averaged probabilities of reaching each round. The group and knockout tabs show one single representative tournament taken from those ten thousand runs, picked to sit near the median chaos level so a typical number of upsets is preserved. This avoids the common failure where a probabilistic table is paired with a modal-bracket view, leaving the two views internally inconsistent.

Top scorer

Top scorer is predicted separately. A player's expected World Cup goals are built from minutes played, recent club scoring rate, role (forward, attacking mid, etc.), and the strength of their domestic league.

League strength is fit empirically. For every past World Cup, the share of top-ten finishers from each league is compared against the share of forwards from that league in tournament squads. Leagues that consistently over-deliver get a multiplier above one; those that under-deliver get a multiplier below one. The fit uses empirical Bayes shrinkage so leagues with few historical samples are pulled toward neutral rather than swinging on small data.

A separate scaler lifts absolute predicted totals so the winner lands in the historical range, not below it.

Validation

The full pipeline is back-tested on sixteen past tournaments (World Cups, Euros, Copa Américas, AFCONs, Asian Cups), running every match through the model using only data available before that match. Each tournament produces around 64 to 128 evaluations; the full grid is roughly 1,400.

The metric is log-loss against the actual result. Changes to the model are kept only if they improve log-loss on the held-out grid, not on the matches they were tuned on. Several intuitively appealing changes (travel fatigue, deeper recency weighting, Bayesian in-tournament updating) were tried and rejected on this basis.

What was deliberately left out

  • Travel fatigue. No measurable effect after controlling for opponent strength.
  • Player form via match-by-match scraping. Noise dominated the signal at the granularity tested.
  • Continental-host edges. Captured already by the home-advantage and altitude layers.
  • Bayesian in-tournament updating. Group-stage results are too few to update ratings reliably; the prior dominates anyway.

Known limitations

  • Top-scorer training data is survivor-biased: it only sees players who actually went to the World Cup. Non-Big-5 leagues have small samples, which is why their multipliers are shrunk hard.
  • The home-advantage figure is averaged across host nations and competitions. A specific host's edge could be larger or smaller.
  • Squad data is only as current as the published rosters. Late injuries are not reflected unless squads are re-scraped.
  • The model has no view on managerial changes, internal squad dynamics, or the specific tactical match-ups that often decide knockout games.

Why the code is private

Parameters and weights are the output of months of validation work. The reasoning above is the part worth sharing.
