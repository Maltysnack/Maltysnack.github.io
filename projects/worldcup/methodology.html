Methodology.

How the World Cup 2026 numbers on the main page are produced.

01Data

Everything the model knows, it learned from results that already happened. No opinions, no bought-in rankings, no eye test.

The backbone is the complete record of senior men's international football: roughly 49,000 matches, every one played since 1872, each carrying date, both teams, score, venue and competition. Sitting alongside the results are more than 47,000 individual goalscorer entries and every international penalty shootout ever recorded, spanning 336 national teams and around 150 distinct competitions, from World Cup finals down to regional cups and friendlies.

Several further sources feed specific layers of the model:

  • Stadium coordinates and elevation for all sixteen World Cup 2026 venues, which drive the altitude adjustment.
  • Published squad lists for the 2026 finalists, scraped per team, each cross-referenced to the player's 2025-26 club season from FBref: minutes, appearances and goals.
  • Top-ten finishers from every previous World Cup, used to fit the league-strength multipliers, never as goal counts directly.

02Team rating: Elo

Every team carries one number for how strong it is right now. Beat someone better than you and it climbs; lose to someone worse and it falls.

The rating belongs to the Elo family used in chess and FiveThirtyEight's old soccer model, but the version here is heavily adapted for international football. All 49,000 matches are replayed in strict chronological order, so a rating is never informed by a match that had not yet happened. Each update is the product of three terms: the surprise of the result against the rating-implied probability, a non-linear margin-of-victory curve that rewards a 4-0 far less than four times a 1-0, and a competition-tier weight that scales a World Cup qualifier well above a mid-cycle friendly. The update rate, the shape of the margin curve, the tier weights and the recency half-life are not set by hand; they are jointly optimised, and several plausible alternatives were discarded for scoring worse out of sample.

# standard Elo update, applied after every match
expected = 1 / (1 + 10 ** ((opp_rating - team_rating) / scale))
team_rating += K * margin_mult * (result - expected)

# K, scale, the margin multiplier and the recency
# half-life are all tuned on held-out data, not shown.

One finding ran against intuition and stuck. Weighting recent results heavily looks like an obvious improvement; in validation it consistently scored worse than a long memory, so the model keeps a long one on purpose.

03Match model

Give the model two teams and it does not just name a winner. It produces a full spread of plausible scorelines, 2-1, 0-0, the occasional 4-0, at realistic frequencies.

A match is drawn from a bivariate scoring distribution with an explicit dependence parameter, not two independent Poisson draws. Independence gets the average goal count right but the shape wrong: too few draws, too few blowouts. Real football goals are correlated, a chasing team commits bodies forward and concedes more, a leading team shuts up shop, and modelling the two score lines jointly, with the correlation itself fitted to observed results, reproduces the real distribution: the draw rate, the 1-0 frequency, the heavy-win tail. That whole distribution, not just the expected winner, is what the knockout simulation depends on.

# one correlated draw, not two independent Poissons
home_goals, away_goals = joint_sample(
    rating_gap,
    home_advantage,   # calibrated from the result database
    altitude_bump,    # high-elevation Mexican venues
    temperature,      # shapes the score distribution
)

Three environmental layers sit on top of the rating gap, each calibrated against the match record rather than set by hand:

  • Home advantage calibrated on the actual home edge measured across the result database.
  • Altitude bump for matches at high-elevation Mexican venues, where visiting sides historically underperform.
  • Temperature on the scoring distribution, fitted so predicted score frequencies match observed ones.

04Squad layer

A nation's rating can drift away from who actually turns up. Stars retire, a wonderkid breaks through, the first-choice striker limps off in May. This layer corrects for that.

It builds a squad-strength index from the current club form of every named player, then blends that index against the team's own rating history. The blend weight is not a guess, it is fitted, and the adjustment is bounded, so a single unusually hot or cold club season cannot overwhelm decades of international evidence.

It is deliberately held out of the live model for now. Adjusting on partial squad lists biases the table toward whichever countries announced first; the layer switches on once enough squads are public for the comparison to be fair.

05Tournament simulation

The model plays the entire World Cup, every group game and every knockout, ten thousand times over. How often a team reaches each stage becomes its probability.

Each run is a complete tournament: all seventy-two group games, standings resolved with the official tie-breakers, the best-third qualification logic, then the thirty-two-team knockout bracket with extra time and a penalty-shootout model fitted on historical shootouts.

for trial in range(10_000):
    standings  = play_group_stage()
    qualifiers = standings + best_thirds(standings)
    champion   = run_knockouts(qualifiers)   # ET, then pens
    record(trial, rounds_reached)

probabilities = average_over_trials()

The Table reports probabilities averaged across all ten thousand runs, a Monte Carlo estimate that tightens as the sample grows. The Group and Knockout tabs instead show one single simulated tournament, selected so its number of upsets sits near the median of the ten thousand. This avoids a subtle but common inconsistency: a bracket stitched together from the most likely result of each individual tie produces a tournament that, taken as a whole, is far more chalk than any real one. Showing a genuine run keeps the probabilistic table and the example bracket telling the same story.

06Top scorer

Who wins the Golden Boot is its own question. The model builds each contender's goal tally from how much he plays, how prolific he is for his club, where he plays, and how strong his league is.

Club scoring rate comes from 2025-26 season data; position sets a role weight. League strength is fitted empirically: for every past World Cup, the share of top-ten finishers from a league is compared with that league's share of forwards in tournament squads. Over-delivering leagues earn a multiplier above one, under-delivering leagues below one, with empirical-Bayes shrinkage so thin samples are pulled toward neutral rather than swinging on a handful of cases.

expected_wc_goals = (
      minutes_share        # how much he is on the pitch
    * club_scoring_rate    # 2025-26 club goals per 90
    * role_weight          # forward, attacking mid, ...
    * league_multiplier    # empirically fitted, per league
    * environment_scaler   # lifts totals into historic range
)
# every weight and multiplier is fitted; values stay private.

Each contender actually receives two independent forecasts, one driven by club form, one by his share of his national team's recent goals, combined with a weight fixed by back-testing against real Golden Boot races. A final environment scaler then lifts absolute totals so the predicted winner lands inside the historical Golden Boot range rather than below it.

07Validation

None of this is worth anything if it cannot be checked. Every change to the model has to prove itself by re-predicting tournaments that have already happened.

The full pipeline is back-tested on sixteen past tournaments, World Cups, Euros, Copa Américas, AFCONs and Asian Cups, by walk-forward cross-validation. For every match the model is refit on only the data that existed before kickoff, predicts, and is scored against what actually happened, roughly 1,400 independent evaluations in all.

loss = 0
for tournament in past_16:   # WCs, Euros, Copas, AFCONs, Asian Cups
    for match in tournament:
        p = model.predict(match, using=data_before(match))
        loss += log_loss(p, actual_result)

# a change ships only if it lowers loss on matches
# it was never tuned on.

The metric is log-loss, the proper scoring rule for probabilistic forecasts: it punishes a confident wrong call far harder than a hedged one. A candidate change ships only if it lowers held-out log-loss, never the log-loss of the matches it was tuned on. It is a deliberately harsh bar, and it is why travel fatigue, deeper recency weighting and Bayesian in-tournament updating were each built, measured, and cut.
