Bauhaus / Chapter 11

The Diaspora

The school closes in 1933. Its faculty scatter. What happens next is the global propagation of what we now call modernism, mediated by Harvard, Chicago, Yale, Black Mountain, Aspen, and, ironically, by the corporate America that the school's Marxist directorship would not have recognised as a friend.

Under Mies van der Rohe the school had relocated from Dessau to Berlin in late 1932, operating in a converted telephone factory under increasing political pressure. In April 1933, two months after the Nazi seizure of power, the Gestapo searched the premises; in August 1933 Mies dissolved the school under conditions the regime would have accepted, ending its institutional life. The school's fourteen years had produced approximately 1,250 students and a faculty whose careers were now interrupted at the worst possible moment.

The faculty diaspora that followed is one of the great forced migrations in 20th-century cultural history. Most of the school's masters were on Nazi blacklists, were Jewish, were Marxist, or were some combination of the three. The ones who could leave generally did. The ones who stayed paid for it. Oskar Schlemmer remained in Germany, denounced and exhibited in the 1937 Degenerate Art exhibition, and died of illness in 1943. Paul Klee, also denounced, retreated to Bern and died there in 1940. Kandinsky settled in Paris and died in 1944, a year before liberation.

The emigrants who reached the United States had outsized influence on what American design education would become. Walter Gropius went briefly to London and then to Harvard, where in 1937 he was appointed chairman of the Graduate School of Design and remade American architectural pedagogy in his image. Mies van der Rohe went to Chicago, took over architecture at the Armour Institute (which became the Illinois Institute of Technology in 1940), and designed the IIT campus while building the most famous American skyscrapers of the post-war era. Laszlo Moholy-Nagy founded the New Bauhaus in Chicago in 1937; after his early death in 1946 it became the Institute of Design, which is now part of IIT. Josef Albers taught at Black Mountain College in North Carolina from 1933 to 1949 and then at Yale until 1958, where his colour theory became the basis for graphic design education in America for two generations. Anni Albers taught alongside him; her work was substantially revived by MoMA in 1949 and is now central to her own canon. Herbert Bayer moved to New York and then to Aspen, where he shaped the Aspen Institute's identity and helped install the European modernist sensibility in American corporate design. Marcel Breuer joined Gropius at Harvard, then opened his own practice and built the Whitney Museum and Saint John's Abbey.

The "Bauhaus look" went global through teaching. Within twenty years of the closure, every elite American design programme either had Bauhaus emigres on faculty or had hired graduates of programmes that did. The Swiss Style and the International Typographic Style of the 1950s and 1960s, the corporate identity work of the 1960s and 1970s, the mid-century modernist furniture market, the IBM logo, the New York subway signage, the Helvetica wayfinding boom: all of these have direct Bauhaus DNA, often two or three teachers deep. The very same forms that began as Volksbedarf in Meyer's Dessau ended up as the visual identity of American capitalism, an irony that nobody seems to have planned and that nobody has unwound since.

Hitchcock and Johnson's 1932 MoMA exhibition Modern Architecture: International Exhibition coined the term International Style a year before the school's closure. By framing European modernism through formal rather than political criteria, it allowed the post-1933 reception of Bauhaus work in America to skip over the politics entirely. The closing chapter of the Bauhaus, then, is the chapter in which the school stops being a school and becomes an idea, deliberately depoliticised, fully exportable, and almost unrecognisable to several of the people who actually built it.

The emigres

  • Walter GropiusLondon (1934-37), then Harvard GSD (1937-52). Founded The Architects Collaborative.
  • Ludwig Mies van der RoheChicago. IIT architecture chair from 1938; designed the IIT campus, Farnsworth House, Crown Hall, Seagram Building.
  • Laszlo Moholy-NagyLondon, then Chicago. Founded the New Bauhaus in 1937; later Institute of Design, now part of IIT. Died 1946.
  • Josef AlbersBlack Mountain College 1933-49, then Yale until 1958. Wrote Interaction of Color (1963); Homage to the Square series 1950-1976.
  • Anni AlbersSame trajectory as Josef. MoMA retrospective 1949. Wrote On Weaving (1965). Major posthumous revival.
  • Herbert BayerNew York, then Aspen from 1946. Shaped the Aspen Institute's identity and the American corporate-design vocabulary.
  • Marcel BreuerLondon, then Harvard with Gropius (1937), then own practice. Whitney Museum (1966), Saint John's Abbey, UNESCO Paris.
  • Hannes MeyerUSSR 1930-36, then Mexico until 1949, then Switzerland. Housing and urban-planning projects across all three.
  • Wassily KandinskyParis from 1933. Continued painting until his death in 1944.
  • Paul KleeBern from 1933. Died 1940. Major late watercolour output.
  • Oskar SchlemmerRemained in Germany. Denounced and exhibited in Degenerate Art (1937). Died 1943.
