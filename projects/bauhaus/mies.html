Mies and the Architectural Exit

Technique

Ludwig Mies van der Rohe took over the Bauhaus directorship in August 1930 after the city of Dessau dismissed Hannes Meyer under political pressure. He was already one of the most prominent modernist architects in Europe; his Barcelona Pavilion (1929) and the Tugendhat House (1928-1930) had established a vocabulary, steel-and-glass minimalism, flowing open plans, materials honestly expressed, that defined what the rest of the century would call high modernism. His arrival at the Bauhaus shifted the school's centre of gravity decisively toward architecture and away from Meyer's sociological programme.

Mies's directorship was politically deescalating in ways the city expected and the students mostly resented. He expelled the communist student cell, reduced the political content of the curriculum, and reorganised the school around individual mastery rather than collective workshop production. The Bauhaus had always been a small school by university standards; under Mies it became smaller and more architectural. In September 1932, when the Nazi-dominated Dessau council voted to close the Bauhaus, Mies relocated it to a disused telephone factory in Berlin-Steglitz as a private institution. It operated there until August 1933, when, after a Gestapo search and increasing pressure, Mies dissolved the school.

The visual output of these final years is mostly architectural drawings: plans, elevations, axonometrics, perspectives. Mies's office produced spare orthogonal renderings, line-only or with minimal hatching, on tracing paper. Students learned the conventions of the architectural drawing as the school's central skill. The aesthetic owes more to engineering drafting than to the painting workshops, which is part of why the Mies period feels different from the rest of the Bauhaus: it stopped looking like art and started looking like a building department.

The generator produces an orthogonal elevation: a building rectangle on a ground line, a regular grid of glass bays, occasional dimension marks. The proportions vary across rolls (some compositions are tall-and-narrow, some long-and-low, some mid-rise). The vocabulary is deliberately limited and the line work is deliberately spare. Nothing here is a Mies-by-Mies; the generator is a Mies-flavoured elevation in the school-of-Mies idiom that he and his students would have produced as classroom exercises.
