Schlemmer's Stage

Geometric mannequins assembled from spheres, cylinders, and trapezoids; the body as architecture, the costume as sculpture. The school's stage workshop produced photographs and performances rather than posters, and lives in a register of its own.

1923 - 1929hybridstage workshop

One to three figures assembled from geometric primitives. Head, torso, arms, legs; each figure a costumed solid. Stage floor and shadows.

Technique

Oskar Schlemmer joined the Bauhaus in 1921, originally as master of mural painting; from 1923 he led the stage workshop (Buhne), the school's most theatrical and least poster-like department. Where the typography workshop produced flat-fill primaries on paper and Kandinsky outlined circles on a dark ground, Schlemmer's workshop produced performances: dance pieces, costumes, set designs, and the photographs that documented them. The body was the medium.

His central thesis, developed in pieces like Mensch und Kunstfigur (Man and Art-Figure, Bauhaus Book 4, 1925), was that the dancing human body should be reconceived as a geometric solid: the head a sphere, the torso a cylinder or trapezoid, the limbs cones or bars. Costumes were therefore architectural shells the dancer inhabited, and choreography became the movement of those shells through stage space. The Triadic Ballet (premiered 1922, performed at the 1923 Bauhaus Exhibition and many times after) is the canonical example: three dancers, three acts, costumes constructed in spheres and disks and skirts of rigid wire.

Schlemmer also painted. Bauhaus Stairway (1932), now at MoMA, shows simplified figures climbing the new Dessau building's stairs in a vertical processional. Tanzerin (Die Geste) and the long Bauhaus Dances series of the late 1920s applied the same body-as-solid reasoning to two-dimensional images. The 1922 Bauhaus signet, the abstract profile-face emblem the school printed on its letterhead, is his design.

The workshop was dissolved by Hannes Meyer in 1929, who considered theatre insufficiently social to justify Bauhaus resources. Schlemmer left and worked in Breslau and Berlin until political pressure made employment impossible; he was denounced by the Nazis in 1937 (works included in the Degenerate Art exhibition) and died of illness in 1943, having spent his last years working covertly on small-scale pieces and on a paint factory in southwestern Germany. He was the major Bauhaus master who stayed.

The generator places one to three figures on a stage. Each figure independently picks one of two schemas. Round uses sphere head, trapezoid torso, bar legs, optional bars-at-angle for arms; closer to the Spherical dancer of the Triadic Ballet's white series. Geometric uses cube head, rectangular torso, blockier limbs, often with a small cuboid hat; closer to the black-series figures of the same ballet. A figure may have an optional sphere-or-cube hat. A horizon line crosses the stage to suggest depth; each figure gets a shadow ellipse beneath. Costume colours are picked per-figure from a Schlemmer-flavoured palette (terracotta, ochre, indigo, charcoal, muted purple, sage, with cream skin). The schemas are rigid by design, in the spirit of his actual mannequin sketches. What it cannot do is movement; Schlemmer's work lived in time, on the stage, and a static SVG cannot stand in for that.
