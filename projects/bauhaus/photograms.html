Bauhaus / Chapter 08

Photograms

Lensless photography. Objects placed directly on light-sensitive paper, then exposed. Where an object blocks light, the paper stays bright; where it passes through, the paper darkens. Moholy-Nagy's preferred medium.

1922 - 1933generatorMoholy-Nagy / Peterhans

Four to nine semi-transparent silhouettes on a dark ground; overlap brightens, simulating multiple light passes through translucent objects.

Technique

A photogram is made without a camera or lens. The photographer lays objects directly on light-sensitive paper or film, exposes the assembly to light, and develops the result. Areas the objects block stay unexposed, reading as light; areas where light reaches darken to black. Translucent objects let some light through, producing intermediate grays. The technique predates the Bauhaus (Henry Fox Talbot called early versions "photogenic drawings" in the 1830s), but the early-1920s revival under Christian Schad, Man Ray, and Laszlo Moholy-Nagy made it a serious modernist medium.

Moholy-Nagy brought the technique to the Bauhaus when he arrived in 1923 and spent the next decade making hundreds of them. For him photograms were not photographs of objects; they were compositions in light, and the camera-free directness of the process made them, in his view, more honest images than the lens-based variety. His Bauhaus Book Painting Photography Film (1925) is the central manifesto: he argued that light was a primary artistic medium, that photography (especially camera-less photography) was the modern visual art, and that painters who refused to engage with mechanical reproduction were nostalgic. The Bauhaus formal photography workshop only opened in 1929 under Walter Peterhans, but Moholy-Nagy's photogram work was continuous from 1923 onward.

Lucia Moholy, Laszlo's wife and a serious photographer in her own right, took the photographs that documented the Dessau Bauhaus building, its rooms, its objects, and its faculty. Her photographs of the building exterior are still the canonical images of the school's architecture; she made them with a large-format view camera and they have nothing in common with photograms beyond the medium. She is the figure the popular history most often skips when crediting "Bauhaus photography" to Laszlo alone.

The generator places four to nine semi-transparent silhouettes (circles, rectangles, narrow bars, occasional thin lines, occasional ellipses) on a near-black ground, drawn in a single layer with the SVG mix-blend-mode: screen compositing model so that overlaps brighten. The effect approximates the way real photograms register two translucent objects: each one blocks some light independently, and where both block, more light gets through, lightening the paper further.

Roughly 40% of rolls also include one or two object-like silhouettes drawn from a small vocabulary (comb, gear, scissors, string-curve), shapes that are recognisable as actual things laid on photographic paper. The original photograms often used everyday objects: scissors, springs, gears, mesh, hands, dust. The compositional restraint is deliberate; photograms were laboratory documents as much as artworks, and the limited vocabulary is part of the look.
