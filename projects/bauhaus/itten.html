Bauhaus / Chapter 01

Itten's Vorkurs

The first three years of the Bauhaus, when Johannes Itten led the preliminary course and the school looked nothing like its later reputation. Robes, breathing exercises, hand-felt forms, mystical pedagogy, dusty palettes; almost no straight lines.

1919 - 1922Weimaressay

Essay

The Bauhaus was founded in Weimar in April 1919, six months after the end of the First World War and four months after the proclamation of the Weimar Republic. Walter Gropius's founding manifesto called for a total integration of art and craft under the unifying discipline of building (Bau): "Architects, sculptors, painters, we all must return to craft! For there is no such thing as 'art by profession.'" The cover image of the manifesto was a woodcut by Lyonel Feininger, a crystalline gothic cathedral piercing the night sky, three stars over its spires. Nothing about that image looks like what we now picture when we say Bauhaus. Nothing about the first three years of the school does either.

The preliminary course, the Vorkurs, was the school's pedagogical foundation: every student took it before specialising. From 1919 to 1923 it was led by Johannes Itten, a Swiss painter and a committed follower of Mazdaznan, a hybrid Zoroastrian-vegetarian spiritual practice that Itten had encountered in Vienna. He wore robes. He had his students wear robes. He led them in breathing exercises before drawing class. He prescribed a Mazdaznan diet of vegetables and garlic for clearer perception. He taught contrast studies (light versus dark, smooth versus rough, warm versus cold), material studies, gesture drawing, and what he called "expression studies" in which students attempted to render a feeling or a piece of music directly onto paper. Measurement and machine were not his vocabulary; intuition and breath were.

Itten's colour theory, eventually published as The Art of Color in 1961, was developed during these years and reflected his pedagogical instincts: colours had spiritual weight; the primaries existed in a structured hierarchy; the colour wheel and the colour star were teaching instruments as much as taxonomies. The hierarchy our Dessau generator now uses, blue 40%, red 30%, yellow 15%, comes from him. The hierarchy was his; the cool industrial flavour with which it was later deployed was not.

Itten was forced out in 1923. Gropius had concluded that for the Bauhaus to survive financially and to fulfil its founding mission, it had to engage seriously with industrial production. Itten's mysticism, his rejection of mechanical reproduction, his cultivation of intuitive feeling, were structurally incompatible with that turn. The replacement, Laszlo Moholy-Nagy, brought the opposite temperament: photographic, mechanical, constructivist, militantly modern. The transition is sometimes told as a clean upgrade. It was not. It was an institutional rupture that removed an entire pedagogical lineage from the school and put another in its place, and the Bauhaus that became famous is the one that lived on after that removal. The Bauhaus before is largely forgotten; this chapter is what it looked like.

Key works

  • Cathedral of Socialism Lyonel Feininger / woodcut / 1919

    Cover of the founding manifesto. Crystalline gothic cathedral with rayed stars over its spires. The visual emblem of the school's first phase.

  • Tower of Fire (Turm des Feuers) Johannes Itten / wooden sculpture / 1920

    Twisted spiral tower in painted wood, climbing toward a flame. Now lost; survives in photographs. Built to express ascent and elemental energy, hallmark Itten themes.

  • Colour Star (Farbkugel) Johannes Itten / diagram / 1921

    Twelve-segmented colour wheel with luminosity gradient. The teaching instrument behind everything our Dessau generator does with colour, two decades before The Art of Color formalised it in print.

  • Expression study (various) Vorkurs students / charcoal, gouache / 1919 - 1922

    Many surviving sheets at the Bauhaus-Archiv. Students drawing a feeling, a piece of music, a season, directly onto paper. Rhythm-and-tension exercises and "Analyses of Old Masters" diagrams.

  • Children's pictures, or the Mountain Johannes Itten / oil on canvas / 1921 - 1922

    Itten's own paintings of the period: muted earth tones, soft-edged geometry, mystical landscape forms. Antithetical to the flat-fill primary aesthetic that would arrive within two years.
