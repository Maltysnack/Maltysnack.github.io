Bauhaus / Chapter 02

The Turn

The single year in which the Bauhaus replaced its preliminary-course master, presented itself to the public, and remade its visual identity. After 1923 the school looked like Bauhaus. Before it did not.

1923Weimaressay

Essay

Two outside pressures had been building on the Weimar Bauhaus since 1921. The first was financial: the school depended on state funding from a Thuringian government that increasingly disliked its experiments. The second was intellectual: Theo van Doesburg, the Dutch De Stijl editor and ideologue, arrived in Weimar in 1922 and set up an unofficial "De Stijl course" in his lodgings just outside the school. He never held a Bauhaus appointment, but he attracted students and faculty to his evenings, and his arguments for geometric clarity, primary colour, and the machine landed with people who had been ready to hear them.

The internal tension between Itten's expressionist Vorkurs and Gropius's industrial ambitions broke in early 1923. Itten resigned. Gropius brought in Laszlo Moholy-Nagy, a Hungarian constructivist who had been working in Berlin and was already known for photograms, photomontages, and a programmatic enthusiasm for industrial materials. Moholy-Nagy took over the Vorkurs and reoriented it: less feeling, more material analysis; less robe, more steel. Within a year the school's centre of gravity had shifted from painting to typography and product design.

That August the school staged its first major public exhibition: the Bauhaus Exhibition of 1923, in Weimar. The slogan, suggested by Gropius for the occasion, was "Kunst und Technik, eine neue Einheit", art and technology, a new unity. It was a deliberate repositioning. The exhibition presented the new Vorkurs material, a model house (the Haus am Horn, built specifically for the show, demonstrating modernist living), Schlemmer's Triadic Ballet performed live, and the iconic poster by the student Joost Schmidt: a stylized geometric head in profile assembled from primary-coloured circles and bars, anchored on a hard diagonal axis. Schmidt's poster did more than any other single image to define what "Bauhaus" looked like for the rest of the school's life.

The same year, Kandinsky distributed his famous shape-and-colour questionnaire among the school: which colour corresponds to which primary shape? The popular distillation of the survey's answers, square-red, circle-blue, triangle-yellow, became the most-cited piece of Bauhaus colour theory ever produced, even though the result is more often cited than agreed on (Kandinsky's own paintings only sometimes follow it). The first Bauhaus Books series, edited by Gropius and Moholy-Nagy, was announced; van Doesburg's Principles of Neo-Plastic Art would appear as Book 6, Moholy-Nagy's Painting, Photography, Film as Book 8, Kandinsky's Point and Line to Plane as Book 9.

By the end of the year the school had what it would not have at the start: a public identity. The cost of that identity was the loss of Itten's lineage and a definitive turn away from the founding manifesto's craft-and-cathedral romanticism. The Bauhaus you came here looking for begins now.

Key works

  • Bauhaus Exhibition Poster Joost Schmidt / lithograph / 1923

    Stylized geometric profile assembled from circles, bars, and half-circles on a hard diagonal axis. The visual definition of "Bauhaus" for most viewers ever since.

  • Haus am Horn Georg Muche, with Bauhaus workshops / built house / 1923

    A model home built for the exhibition. Square plan, central living space, modular kitchen by Marcel Breuer. The first realised demonstration of the school's industrial-design ambitions.

  • Triadic Ballet (Das triadische Ballett) Oskar Schlemmer / dance work / premiered 1922, performed at the 1923 exhibition

    Three dancers in geometric costumes assembled from spheres, cones, and disks. The stage workshop's signature work and a totally different visual register from posters or paintings.

  • Shape-and-Colour Questionnaire Wassily Kandinsky / printed survey / 1923

    A short questionnaire asking respondents to pair the three primary colours with the three primary shapes. The most-cited piece of Bauhaus colour theory, although the results were less unanimous than the popular version suggests.

  • Universal (Universal-Alphabet, in development) Herbert Bayer / typeface / begun 1923, completed 1925

    Lowercase-only geometric sans-serif assembled from circles, half-circles, and bars. The typographic counterpart to the new visual programme; foundational for what became known as "Bauhaus typography".
