Klee's Room

Magic-square grids in dusty pinks, sages, ochres, and muted blues. Paul Klee's late-Bauhaus mode, where the rules are clear but the hand stays visible. A hybrid room: the generator captures the structure, an SVG filter fakes the bleed, and the essay carries the rest.

1921 - 1931hybridpainting workshop

Non-uniform grid of muted cells, light edge-displacement to suggest bleed, occasional cross, X, or dot as motif.

Technique

Paul Klee joined the Bauhaus in 1921 as master of the bookbinding workshop, then of stained glass, then mural painting; from 1922 he taught his own classes on form theory and pictorial composition. He stayed until 1931, when he left for Dusseldorf. His pedagogical writings, especially Pedagogical Sketchbook (Bauhaus Book 2, 1925) and the unfinished Notebooks, are among the most lucid art-education texts of the 20th century: composition explained as rule, with worked examples.

Klee's own paintings live in a register that no other Bauhaus master shared. He worked in watercolour, gouache, oil-and-watercolour transfer, and ink, on prepared papers and canvases. His palette was muted: dusty pinks, sage greens, ochres, soft browns, muted purples, washed blues. The primaries the typography workshop relied on barely appear. In his late Bauhaus years (roughly 1928 onwards) he developed what is sometimes called his "magic-square" mode: Polyphony (1932), Ad Parnassum (1932), New Harmony (1936), in which the whole canvas is a grid of small colour cells with subtle tonal relationships. Those works are the structural part of his output that a generator can plausibly approach.

The generator alternates between two modes per roll. The magic-square mode builds a near-uniform non-uniform grid of four to six columns by three to five rows, fills each cell with a colour drawn from a muted six-tone palette, and applies a slight per-cell tonal variation so adjacent cells of the "same" colour are not identical. The Polyphony mode uses five to eight tall vertical bands of varied width, each divided into two to four horizontal segments; the vertical axis dominates and the rhythm of bands recalls the musical analogy Klee made explicit in Polyphony (1932). Both modes share the palette, the per-segment tonal variation, the twenty-to-thirty-percent motif density (cross, X, dot, tiny triangle, short arrow) drawn in a darker ink, and an SVG turbulence-and-displacement filter applied to the whole composition that nods at watercolour bleed without literally simulating it.

What the generator misses is large. Klee's paintings often imply figures: Senecio (1922) is composed entirely of geometric primitives but unmistakably a face; Twittering Machine (1922) renders bird-like creatures on a crank; Cat and Bird (1928) suggests animal silhouettes through colour fields. That figurative-from-geometric tension is the centre of his work, and a rule-based generator cannot find it without a great deal more cleverness than this one has. The grid mode is what we can do. The metaphorical readings are what live in the essay. The room is honest about the split.
