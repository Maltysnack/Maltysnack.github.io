Kandinsky's Room

Outlined circles and ellipses overlapping on a near-black ground, with diagonal lines cutting the field. The painting workshop's rule-based abstraction; the school's most-explicit theorist of composition was also its most generatable painter.

Technique

Wassily Kandinsky joined the Bauhaus in 1922 as master of the mural workshop and stayed until the school's closure in 1933. He had already published Concerning the Spiritual in Art in 1911, which made him one of European modernism's most-cited theorists of abstraction; at the Bauhaus he expanded the theoretical work into form theory, culminating in Point and Line to Plane (Bauhaus Book 9, 1926). His Bauhaus paintings are some of the school's most recognisable output: outlined circles overlapping on dark grounds, weights and tensions arranged with a structural rigour that, on inspection, was as rule-based as any typography page.

The Kandinsky room does not look like the typography workshop. Where Bayer and Moholy-Nagy worked on a paper ground with flat-filled primaries, Kandinsky worked on a near-black ground with outlined shapes; the outlines stay visible through overlap, creating an X-ray effect where layered forms remain legible. The colour palette is wider, with primaries joined by greens, violets, oranges, and a cream that doubles as outline material. The basic units are circles and ellipses more than squares; the typography workshop's bar is replaced by the diagonal line cutting the field.

Kandinsky was the most explicit rule-codifier of all the Bauhaus masters. Point and Line to Plane is, almost literally, a textbook on generating composition from first principles. He argued that the basic graphic elements (point, line, plane) carry intrinsic weights and tensions that the artist arranges the way an engineer arranges forces. A famous (and disputed) 1923 questionnaire he distributed at the school asked respondents to associate each primary colour with a primary shape; the result, square-red, circle-blue, triangle-yellow, became the popular shorthand for "Bauhaus" colour theory, although Kandinsky's own paintings only sometimes obey it.

The generator places five to nine shapes on a warm near-black ground, predominantly circles, with ellipses, triangles, and occasionally a concentric pairing (an outline circle around a smaller filled one) as a strong accent. Every shape gets a thin cream outline so overlaps remain readable. One to three thin diagonal lines cross the field behind the shapes. This captures Kandinsky's formal language. It does not capture the spiritual dimension he believed art could reach, which is the half of his work that lives in text rather than rules. The room has both.
