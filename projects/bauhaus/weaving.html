The Weaving Workshop

Run by Gunta Stolzl from 1925, Anni Albers from 1931. The largest workshop by enrolment, almost entirely women, long dismissed and recently reappraised as one of the most rigorous abstract design programmes the school produced. Loom logic, banded structure, earth palettes.

1919 - 1933generatorStolzl / Albers / Berger

A non-uniform grid of cells; each column has a dominant colour and cells lean toward it.

Technique

The weaving workshop was founded in Weimar in 1919 as a small adjunct, moved to Dessau with the rest of the school in 1925, and was led for most of its productive years by Gunta Stolzl, with Anni Albers taking over briefly from 1931 until the school's closure. It was the largest workshop by student enrolment, the most financially successful (it sold textiles to outside clients to keep the school running), and the most heavily female. Otti Berger, who was murdered in Auschwitz in 1944, ran the weaving research programme; her industrial fabric designs are now in major museum collections.

Mid-century histories of the school largely skipped the workshop. The accepted story treated weaving as craft rather than design, the women's territory rather than the school's serious work. Scholarship from the 1990s onward has comprehensively reversed this; Anni Albers's MoMA retrospective (1949 originally, with later revivals) and recent monographs (T'ai Smith especially) treat the workshop as a parallel abstract programme to the painting workshops, not a lesser one.

The visual logic comes from the loom itself. Warp threads run vertically, weft threads run horizontally, and the weave structure determines how they meet. The result is naturally banded and cellular: every weaving is a non-uniform grid before any decisions are made about colour. The workshop's masters built their abstract vocabulary on this scaffold: vertical columns of colour, horizontal divisions, gradient bands, occasional diamond or chevron motifs. The palette was earth, not primary: unbleached linen as the ground, with indigo, rust, ochre, charcoal, and muted greens as the working colours.

Level 1: Grid. A non-uniform grid of six to ten columns by five to eight rows, snapped to 20-pixel units. Each column is assigned a dominant colour from the palette, and every cell within the column has a 70% chance of using that dominant (with a weighted random from the full palette otherwise). One or two small diamond motifs occasionally land as accent. This is the loom-as-grid mode, closest to Anni Albers's printed pictographic series and to Stolzl's gridded wall hangings.

Level 2: Stripes. Pure vertical bands. Six to twelve stripes of varied widths span the full height of the canvas, each in a single colour from the palette. No horizontal divisions, no cell structure; this is the loom showing only its warp. Occasionally a stripe is "interrupted" by a short horizontal weft band of contrasting colour, mimicking the way a weaver lays a feature thread across selected warps. Closer to Stolzl's mid-1920s stripe wall hangings and to Anni Albers's Wall Hanging (Black White Yellow), 1926, in its mature, simplified form.

Level 3: Motifs. The grid from Level 1, but with motif density turned up significantly: four to nine motifs (diamonds, chevrons, crosses, small triangles) drawn inside randomly chosen cells, with a small chance of a row-aligned chevron sequence spanning multiple adjacent cells. This is the motif-heavy mode that shows up in Anatolian-influenced and Navajo-influenced exercises that some Bauhaus weavers explicitly studied: structure plus repeated geometric ornament.
