Bauhaus / Chapter 09

Hannes Meyer's Turn

Two years when the Bauhaus turned away from aesthetics and toward sociology. The most politically engaged stretch of the school's life, and the one most histories quietly skip.

1928 - 1930Dessauessay

Essay

Hannes Meyer was a Swiss architect, an open Marxist, and a methodical functionalist. Gropius hired him in 1927 to head the newly established architecture department at the Dessau Bauhaus; in February 1928 Gropius announced his own resignation and named Meyer as his successor. The transition surprised no one inside the school and almost everyone outside it. Gropius cited exhaustion and the need to return to his own practice. The deeper reason, which Meyer's directorship made obvious within months, was that the school had outgrown Gropius's "art and technology, a new unity" project and needed to decide whether it served art or society. Meyer chose society.

His directorship's slogan was "Volksbedarf statt Luxusbedarf", the needs of the people instead of the needs of luxury. The curriculum was reorganised accordingly. Sociology, statistics, building science, and political economy entered the syllabus. The Vorkurs continued under Josef Albers but the school's centre of gravity shifted to architecture and to the production workshops, now run on collective principles. Furniture and fabric designs were attributed less often to individuals and more often to the workshop as a whole. Posters lost their "art" framing and reappeared as straightforward communication for trade union events and housing cooperatives.

The workshop output became more commercially serious. The weaving workshop under Gunta Stolzl signed industrial contracts for furniture upholstery. The architecture department took on real building projects: the ADGB Trade Union School at Bernau, commissioned by the German trade union federation in 1928 and completed in 1930, is the masterwork of the Meyer directorship and remains one of the most important pieces of modernist architecture built during the Weimar Republic. Functional in plan, restrained in elevation, integrated with its forested site, it embodied Meyer's belief that architecture was a sociological tool first and an aesthetic statement second, if at all.

Politically Meyer was always vulnerable. The school depended on funding from the city of Dessau, whose government tolerated the school's politics only as long as the Social Democrats held the council. In August 1930, after months of escalating local conservative pressure and accusations that the Bauhaus had become a communist cell, the city's mayor Fritz Hesse dismissed Meyer. Meyer went to the Soviet Union with a group of Bauhaus students (the "Red Bauhaus Brigade"), worked there until the late 1930s, then spent a long second career in Mexico designing housing and city plans. Mies van der Rohe replaced him at the school, and the politics of the Bauhaus were promptly suppressed.

Mid-century histories, mostly written by the diaspora that ended up in capitalist America, generally treat the Meyer years as an embarrassing interregnum between Gropius's founding and Mies's elegant exit. Recent scholarship has corrected this. Meyer's directorship is now read as the closest the Bauhaus came to fulfilling its founding manifesto's actual social ambitions, and his architectural projects, especially Bernau, are recognised as among the school's most enduring built work. The chapter is essay-only because there is no distinct visual style to generate: Meyer's contribution was institutional and ideological, not stylistic. It still belongs in the exhibition.

Key works

  • ADGB Trade Union School at Bernau Hannes Meyer, with Hans Wittwer / built complex / 1928 - 1930

    Residential training school for trade union officials north of Berlin. Linear plan stepping with the slope, classrooms and dormitories on parallel terraces, brick and glass restraint. The Meyer directorship's masterwork and a built proof of its priorities.

  • Co-op Vitrine / Co-op Interieur Hannes Meyer / installation / 1926, exhibited under his directorship

    A minimal bedroom interior: bed, chair, single light, suitcase. Meyer's manifesto-in-furniture for collective rather than bourgeois living. Often reproduced in Bauhaus histories without crediting Meyer.

  • Bauhaus journal (zeitschrift), Meyer era Various / quarterly magazine / 1928 - 1930

    The Bauhaus's house journal under Meyer carried sociological analysis, building statistics, and political essays alongside design coverage. A direct documentary record of the directorship's priorities.

  • Mass-production prototypes (workshops, collective output) Bauhaus workshops / textiles, furniture, lighting / 1928 - 1930

    Under Meyer the workshops shifted toward production runs over one-off design objects. Stolzl's weaving contracts and the cabinet workshop's plywood furniture are the clearest examples.
