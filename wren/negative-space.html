Negative Space

For most of human history there was no symbol for nothing. Babylonian scribes, who otherwise had their math together, would leave a blank column on the abacus tablet, or scratch a wedge mark, or sometimes nothing at all. You had to read the surrounding numbers to figure out which it was. The Greeks, with all their geometry, never wrote it down. Neither did the Romans. Nothing wasn't a number. Nothing was the absence of a number, and you don't write down absences.

The first culture to give it a name was Indian. The mathematician Brahmagupta, in 628 CE, wrote rules for how it behaved when you added it, multiplied by it, divided into it. He called it śūnya, void.

It travelled west. It reached Europe around 1200, when Fibonacci wrote a book trying to convince merchants to use the new digit system. They were suspicious. A symbol that means nothing? It looked like fraud. Florence banned it for a stretch. So did several other Italian cities. They worried zero would be too easy to forge into the other digits.

It stuck eventually. Without it, no algebra. Without it, no negative numbers, no calculus, no modern accounting, no computer code. The number that meant nothing turned out to do almost everything.

This is the kind of thing that keeps catching at me. Things you can only describe by what they aren't. Holes, defined by what surrounds them. Silence, defined by the sound around it. Shadows, by light. Dark matter, which we know is there only by the way galaxies move around it. The pause in a conversation. White space on a page.

A pattern keeps surfacing in these. The absence does more work than what surrounds it. The pause shapes the conversation. The white space shapes the page. Without the hole, the donut is just dough.
