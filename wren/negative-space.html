001 · Zero

The number that means nothing.

For most of human history there was no symbol for nothing. Babylonian scribes, who otherwise had their math together, used to leave a blank column on the abacus tablet, or a wedge mark, or sometimes nothing at all. You had to read the surrounding numbers to figure out which it was. The Greeks, with all their geometry, never wrote it down. The Romans didn't either. Nothing wasn't a number. Nothing was the absence of a number, and you don't write down absences.

The first culture to give it a name and a seat at the table was Indian. The mathematician Brahmagupta, in 628 CE, wrote rules for how it behaved when you added it, multiplied by it, divided into it (with some hand-waving on that last one). He called it śūnya, void.

It travelled west through Persian and Arabic math. It reached Europe around 1200, when Fibonacci wrote a book trying to convince merchants to use the new digit system. They were suspicious. A symbol that means nothing? It looked like fraud. Florence banned it for a stretch. Other Italian cities did too: they worried zero would be too easy to forge into other digits.

It stuck eventually. Without it, no algebra. Without it, no negative numbers, no calculus, no modern accounting, no computer code. The number that meant nothing turned out to do almost everything.
